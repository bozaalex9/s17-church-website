# S17 Motion Bible 3.0

## Editorial Cinematic Motion

The scroll is the camera. The page is the story. The objects are the witnesses. Motion guides the stranger toward family.

Motion must proclaim rather than decorate. It may be energetic, cinematic, and memorable, but it must remain ordered and emotionally intelligible.

S17's motion energy should surpass Spotify's energy, but with gospel gravity. This is a standard of aliveness and choreography, not permission to copy Spotify's design or commercial posture.

## Core rule

Use motion as generously as the story requires. The goal is not minimal motion; the goal is meaningful motion.

The approved vocabulary is not a collection of rare exceptions. Fade-ups, confident card entrances, sliding card layers, overlapping scenes, fading and scaling typography, editorial image reveals, parallax, cinematic transitions, and Spotify-like scene choreography may be used throughout the site whenever they make the story clearer, more emotional, and more memorable.

Do not limit this vocabulary out of fear. Do not flatten S17 into repeated basic fade-ups or make the church feel less alive than a commercial product page. Every use must remain directed, composed, readable, performant, accessible, responsive, gospel-centered, and emotionally purposeful.

## Choreography

- Build one emotional idea per scene.
- Connect scenes through overlap and handoff rather than routine hard breaks.
- Reveal in meaningful order: dominant typography, witness photography, supporting copy, editorial object, then CTA.
- Give the hero the greatest movement; photography follows; editorial objects and cards carry the next level; readable typography stays stable; controls respond quickly.
- Use pause as rhythm: arrival, proclamation, comprehension, invitation, handoff.
- Allow expressive pathway and story cards to lift, settle, overlap, and carry controlled spring energy.
- Keep doctrine, Scripture, care, forms, and practical details calmer.

## Full editorial cinematic vocabulary

### Fade-ups

Fade-ups are foundational and may be used throughout an editorial composition. Combine them with stagger, scale, opacity, masking, scene overlap, image depth, CTA timing, or card movement so repeated entrances still feel choreographed rather than templated.

### Cards that pop into place

Cards may arrive with confident scale, spring, lift, or depth. Use this for pathway cards, story cards, Gather / Grow / Go, campaigns, youth moments, documentary galleries, ministry invitations, and homepage editorial objects. The effect should feel premium and intentional, never cheap or cartoonish.

### Cards sliding over one another

Cards may overlap and slide as an editorial stack when motion communicates sequence, depth, or progression. Appropriate uses include discipleship pathways, testimony sequences, city witness cards, ministry moments, and cinematic homepage scenes. Layering must express story order rather than random movement.

### Overlapping scenes

Scene overlap is a core behavior. The default handoff is:

1. Scene A
2. Scene A plus the beginning of Scene B
3. Scene B

The next scene may introduce its tone, image, typography, or editorial object before the current scene fully exits. The page should feel filmed rather than stacked.

### Typography fading while scaling

Hero statements, “Seen. Sanctified. Sent.”, “We gather. We grow. We go.”, theological declarations, and scene openings may fade while subtly scaling. The movement must settle before sustained reading; readable text stays stable once comprehension is expected.

### Editorial image reveals

Photography is witness. Mask reveals, image depth, push-ins, layered entrances, scroll-linked reveals, and overlap with typography are approved when they strengthen documentary testimony.

### Parallax

Parallax is part of the core language for heroes, documentary imagery, environmental layers, editorial transitions, and scene depth. It should create physical continuity without becoming a theme-park effect.

### Cinematic transitions

Major handoffs may behave like editorial cuts, camera travel, scene overlap, or tonal shifts. Use them for homepage progression, About identity, Gather / Grow / Go, mission witness, and campaigns. Avoid generic template effects.

### Spotify-like scene choreography

Scenes may flow continuously, begin before the prior scene ends, introduce cards and objects with confidence, build visual energy, and guide visitors toward a next step. Translate that choreographic confidence into gospel storytelling; do not copy Spotify's branding, visual identity, or commercial posture.

## Proclamation revision

“Subtle by default” and “rare expressive exception” are not governing guidance. Motion intensity and frequency should follow meaning. Joyful worship, testimony, belonging, baptism, mission, and invitation may receive confident and repeated cinematic movement. Stillness is reserved for moments that genuinely need reverence or comprehension.

## Guardrails

- Native scrolling only; no scroll hijacking.
- Transform and opacity first.
- No essential content inside canvas.
- No global WebGL or decorative ambient loops.
- Compose concurrent effects into a clear hierarchy; remove only effects that compete for attention or obscure meaning.
- Reduced motion removes camera travel, parallax, flips, bounce, dramatic lift, pointer depth, and ambient movement while preserving the same complete story.
- Mobile uses shorter distances, stacked scenes, stable reading text, visible tap-accessible content, and clear CTAs.

## Final standard

Use motion generously, cinematically, editorially, and with energy. Never use it as empty decoration. Every movement should help a visitor feel:

- I am being guided.
- This story matters.
- This church is alive.
- There is a place for me.
- There is a next step.

Motion is cinematic storytelling. Motion directs attention, creates emotional continuity, and guides people from stranger to family.
